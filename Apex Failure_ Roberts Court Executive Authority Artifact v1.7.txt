Apex Failure
The Roberts Court and the Uneven Workmanship of Constitutional Reset
I. Introduction — Binding Law and the Route Back to the Human
The Roberts Court’s executive-power cases do not show a Court incapable of building constitutional routes. They show something more troubling: a Court that can build routes, but leaves them thinnest where the person governed by the reset most needs law to carry them.
That is what Apex Failure means here.
It does not mean that the Supreme Court always fails. It does not mean that the Court lacks constitutional authority, that every executive-power holding is wrong, or that the Court never supplies usable law. The Roberts Court has identified genuine constitutional problems involving divided responsibility, unreviewable executive decisionmaking, administrative adjudication, removal, and presidential office-function. In some parts of that architecture, it has also supplied real routes through which the constitutional system can carry the change.
Apex Failure names the narrower failure that remains: a binding constitutional reset can carry the institution, office, doctrine, or constitutional category more successfully than the human being governed by it.
The distinction matters because Supreme Court decisions do more than resolve the dispute between the parties before the Court. Some decisions materially change who may exercise public authority, who may remove or supervise an officer, which institution may issue a final decision, which tribunal may adjudicate a claim, what evidence may be considered, what remedy may follow, and which forms of public conduct remain subject to ordinary legal process. These decisions reset constitutional relations. Once the Court acts, lower courts, Congress, agencies, officers, litigants, and persons already living under the new rule must begin carrying that reset immediately.
Sometimes the Court shows what responsible carrying work looks like.
In United States v. Arthrex, Inc., 594 U.S. 1 (2021), administrative patent judges could issue decisions that became the final answer of the Executive Branch without review by a superior officer constitutionally accountable for the result.
The Court identified that finality as the defect and supplied a remedy fitted to it: the Director of the Patent and Trademark Office could review the decisions and issue the Executive Branch’s final answer.
The repair did not resolve every possible question about how that review should operate. But it made the constitutional relationship legible. The Court identified the missing function, assigned it to an officer capable of performing it, and permitted the institution to continue under the repaired arrangement.
That example establishes an important baseline. The Court can do more than announce a constitutional objection and leave everyone else to improvise around it. It can identify the work placed under pressure and provide a usable legal route through which that work can continue.
The problem is that this level of workmanship is not distributed evenly.
The Court often gives clearer routes where the burden can be framed as a clean institutional question. An officer may be made removable. A superior may be given review. A particular adjudication may be routed to an Article III court and jury. A distinctive institution may receive a cause standard, process, and judicial review. A constitutional rule may be stated clearly enough to govern ordinary administration.
But constitutional law does not operate only through institutions.
It reaches persons.
A person governed by public authority may need to know who acted, under what legal power, and according to which classification. That person may need notice, an opportunity to respond, access to evidence, reasons, review, and a remedy fitted to the violation. A defendant may need to know what conduct and evidence remain available to criminal process. A removed officeholder may need more than recognition that the governing structure was unlawful. A regulated party may need to know which forum can hear the claim. A lower court may need enough guidance to apply the rule without waiting for the Supreme Court to define the decisive category again.
These are not moral embellishments placed around constitutional law.
They are legal routes.
They are how the person affected by public power remains present inside the system that governs them.
An institution may survive while those routes fail. An office may be protected while evidence becomes unavailable for lawful use. A constitutional violation may be recognized while meaningful remedy remains out of reach. Political accountability may remain possible while no court or tribunal can provide the particular legal answer the injury requires. The machinery of government may continue moving while the person inside it becomes delayed, thinned, or lost.
That is the central asymmetry examined here.
The Roberts Court’s executive-power architecture is not adequately described as a simple doctrine of presidential supremacy. Nor is it merely a collection of unrelated conservative outcomes. Across removal, supervision, executive review, administrative adjudication, judicial process, and presidential immunity, the Court has repeatedly decided which institution or officer should carry a constitutional function and under what conditions that authority may be exercised.
The serious question begins after the reassignment.
When the Court invalidates an institutional arrangement, narrows an inherited doctrine, changes the forum, protects official conduct, or reallocates final authority, what happens to the lawful work the former arrangement had been performing? Who preserves process, evidence, continuity, remedy, and record? Can the people and institutions already governed by the new rule proceed without reconstructing the Court’s category for themselves?
The answer does not depend on proving why any Justice acted. Institutional workmanship can be judged from the structure the Court released and the burdens it left behind. The relevant evidence is what the decision protects, what it displaces, what it assigns elsewhere, and what the legal system must absorb when the route runs out.
The contrast is most exposed in two places.
Presidential immunity shows what happens when protection of the office places extraordinary pressure on the legal treatment of mixed conduct, evidence, criminal process, and public record. Removal remedy shows the same problem in a more mature field: the Court may recognize a structural violation and repair presidential control for the future while leaving the officer or claimant who bore the violation with only a narrow path to consequence.
Together, those cases expose the failure at issue.
The Roberts Court can build constitutional architecture.
It builds it unevenly.
And its workmanship becomes thinnest where constitutional law must carry the person governed by power through the routes by which power remains answerable.
II. Binding Force and the Work a Constitutional Reset Creates
A Supreme Court decision does not become optional because its reasoning or consequences remain incomplete.
Its holding binds. Lower courts must apply it. Public institutions must reorganize around it where necessary. Congress, agencies, officers, litigants, and persons affected by the rule must treat the new legal arrangement as operative.
None of the argument here denies that authority.
But authority and completed workmanship answer different questions.
Binding force asks whether the legal system must treat the holding as law.
Workmanship asks what law the Court has given everyone else to proceed with. This is not offered as a separate cause of action or an independent rule of decision. It is the professional standard this artifact uses to evaluate whether the Court has completed the architecture-setting work its own binding judgment creates. 
Those questions can produce different answers. A holding may govern immediately while leaving the boundary of its rule unclear. A constitutional category may have an obvious central example while the foreseeable mixed cases remain difficult to classify. An institutional structure may be invalidated while the treatment of earlier actions, reliance, or remedies remains uncertain. An office may be protected from legal process while the status of evidence touching both protected and unprotected conduct remains unresolved.
The rule is still law.
The transition may still be poorly carried.
That distinction is easiest to miss when the Court states a strong constitutional principle. Once the Court says that an officer must be removable, that an inferior officer’s decision must be reviewable, that a punitive claim must reach an Article III court and jury, or that a category of presidential conduct receives constitutional protection, the clarity of the central rule can make the Court’s work appear complete.
But a constitutional reset is not exhausted by its cleanest proposition.
The Court’s executive-power cases repeatedly assign different constitutional functions to different actors. Removal connects executive administration to presidential supervision and political responsibility. Superior review ensures that the Executive Branch’s final decision belongs to a constitutionally accountable officer. Article III and jury decisions protect forms of adjudication that cannot be finalized inside an agency. Presidential immunity shields the exercise of protected presidential functions from forms of criminal process capable of distorting the office itself.
These relations are not interchangeable.
Removal is not the same as review. Review is not the same as impartial adjudication. Political accountability is not the same as judicial remedy. Protection from prosecution is not the same as a rule governing every evidentiary use of official conduct. A person may know who is politically responsible for government action and still lack a hearing, access to evidence, review, or relief.
The Court’s project is therefore more serious than the claim that it simply prefers presidential power. It is deciding where constitutional work belongs.
That is also why the work can be judged.
When the Court changes who may exercise authority or where a claim may proceed, the legal system needs more than a slogan announcing the favored institution. It ordinarily needs a usable rule and a meaningful boundary. Where predictable disputes will combine protected and unprotected elements, lower actors need some method for separating them. Where the Court displaces an existing arrangement, the law must account for the actions, records, expectations, and injuries already formed under it. And where public power reaches a person, that person needs some legally recognizable route through which the classification can be challenged and the consequence answered.
The Court need not resolve every possible application in advance.
Constitutional law develops through cases. New institutional forms arise. Facts combine in unexpected ways. Lower courts properly perform much of the work of applying doctrine. Congress may redesign statutes. Agencies may reconstruct procedures. Some boundaries become visible only after experience exposes them.
Uncertainty is not itself failure.
A doctrine may remain lawfully open where the Court decides the case before it, gives lower actors a usable rule, identifies the relevant constitutional boundary, and leaves genuinely new questions for later disputes. Difficulty does not prove that the Court has neglected its role. Nor does future litigation, standing alone, show that the original decision was deficient.
The workmanship inquiry does not turn on inadvertence. A constitutional choice may be deliberate, binding, and justified at its core while still under-carrying the legal routes needed to administer the boundary it creates. Intentional design and adequate successor carry are different questions. 
But the appeal to case-by-case adjudication has a limit.
When the Court’s own judgment makes a new constitutional arrangement immediately operative, the foreseeable burdens created by that arrangement are no longer hypothetical merely because the opinion does not decide them.
Someone must act next.
An agency must restructure. A lower court must classify conduct. A prosecutor must determine what remains chargeable and provable. An officer must know whether a removal is effective. Congress must determine what institutional design remains available. A litigant must decide where to file. A person already governed by the former arrangement must determine whether the recognized constitutional defect carries any remedy.
The Court may leave truly novel questions open. It cannot make a binding change and then treat the immediate work created by that change as though no one must carry it until the Court returns.
That is not a demand for a constitutional code.
The difference between a code and competent judicial workmanship is substantial. A code attempts to dictate applications in advance. Workmanship gives legally responsible actors enough orientation to perform their own roles. It identifies the relevant rule, the kind of boundary at stake, the institution authorized to decide, and the process through which a disputed application may be tested.
The Court can leave the factual classification to a lower court while explaining what matters to the classification. It can preserve an exception without deciding every possible analogy while identifying why the exception exists. It can remand a remedy question while stating the legal relation that should govern it. It can allow doctrine to develop without forcing everyone below the Court to guess which unspoken feature of the last case controlled the result.
The distinction is between development and reconstruction.
Development begins from law that can travel.
Reconstruction occurs when lower actors must reverse-engineer the Court’s decisive category while the category already governs them.
That burden becomes especially serious because the Court acts upon an existing legal order, not an empty field. The arrangements it invalidates may have carried useful and lawful functions even if their constitutional form was defective. An agency may have preserved expertise and continuity. A tenure provision may have protected a form of decisional independence. A prior remedial rule may have supplied a route for people already injured. An inherited doctrine may have organized conduct and reliance for years.
Holding the arrangement unconstitutional does not prove that everything it carried was constitutionally worthless.
The Court may properly remove the defective structure. But the lawful work performed through that structure must still be preserved, transferred, narrowed, or consciously left behind. Otherwise constitutional correction can strengthen one relationship while silently erasing another.
A removal decision may strengthen presidential supervision while leaving the claimant unable to show what the defective structure did to them. An immunity decision may protect presidential judgment while leaving courts without a workable method for presenting the unprotected part of an alleged course of conduct. A forum decision may vindicate Article III while leaving unresolved which adjacent claims remain administratively adjudicable. A narrow institutional exception may protect the body before the Court while giving comparable institutions little guidance about whether the same relation applies to them.
In each setting, the Court may have identified a real constitutional problem.
The workmanship question is what happens after the Court acts upon it.
That inquiry is not a referendum on the intelligence, sincerity, or political identity of the Justices. A court may correctly identify a constitutional defect and still fail to carry the transition adequately. It may protect a legitimate office-function and leave the perimeter of the protection too thin. It may lawfully overrule precedent while failing to account for work the precedent had been performing. It may strengthen political responsibility while weakening another route of legal answer.
Correctness and workmanship overlap, but they are not the same judgment.
A preferred outcome does not excuse poor construction. An unwelcome outcome does not prove it.
The question is whether the rule released by the Court can be carried by competent actors beyond the case’s cleanest facts—and whether the persons already governed by it remain present inside the legal process.
Constitutional law makes offices, branches, jurisdictions, and powers legible, but it becomes government through what those institutions do to people. The standard is not victory. A person may lose and still have been carried by law. The standard is lawful presence: a route to appear, contest the classification, test the government’s account, preserve a record, and receive an answer fitted to the legal burden.
The Court’s own decisions show that this is possible. They establish the benchmark—and make the unevenness harder to excuse.
III. The Court Can Carry
The Roberts Court’s own decisions show that this standard is achievable. It sometimes identifies the constitutional work placed under pressure and supplies a route fitted to that work. 
Arthrex remains the clearest example.
The constitutional defect was not administrative adjudication in the abstract. Nor was it simply that inferior officers exercised substantial federal authority. The problem was that administrative patent judges could issue decisions that became the Executive Branch’s final answer without review by an officer possessing the constitutional status to own that result.
The Court’s remedy addressed that precise relation. The Director of the Patent and Trademark Office received authority to review the decisions and issue the final executive judgment.
The institution survived. The adjudicatory work continued. But finality moved to an officer capable of bearing constitutional responsibility for it.
The repair did not answer every procedural question surrounding Director review. It did not specify every reason that must be given, every record that must be considered, or every limit governing revision of a subordinate decision. It did something more basic and more important: it made the constitutional correction usable. The agency knew where final authority now rested. The parties knew that the subordinate decision was reviewable. Lower courts could identify the relationship the Constitution required without recreating the doctrine from its premises.
The defect and the remedy spoke to one another.
That is what competent constitutional repair looks like.
The Court has also shown that it can protect a legal route for the person subjected to government power.
In SEC v. Jarkesy, 603 U.S. 109 (2024), the government sought civil penalties for securities fraud through an internal administrative proceeding. The Court held that the relevant claim carried the Seventh Amendment jury right and could not receive final punitive judgment solely inside the agency.
The significance of the decision is not confined to the abstract allocation of authority between agencies and courts. It changes what process the person facing the penalty receives. The government must establish the covered claim in an Article III court, through a proceeding in which the accused party may contest the evidence and receive a jury determination where the Constitution requires one.
The constitutional protection arrives through a forum.
The later decision in FCC v. AT&T, Inc., Nos. 25-406 & 25-567, 608 U.S. ___ (June 4, 2026),  sharpened the institutional design line. There, the agency’s order did not conclusively determine the parties’ legal obligations. Before a carrier could be compelled to pay, the government had to bring a civil enforcement action in federal court, where the defendant could obtain de novo adjudication and insist that a jury make the ultimate determination of disputed facts. The agency’s prior factual findings were not conclusive.
The distinction gave Congress and agencies something they could use.
An agency may investigate, apply expertise, form a preliminary view, and initiate enforcement without necessarily displacing Article III and the jury. The constitutional difficulty changes when the agency’s own determination becomes the final source of punitive legal consequence. A genuine judicial proceeding, in which the government must prove its case anew, preserves a different relation.
That is not merely a declaration that one arrangement is unconstitutional.
It is guidance for constructing another.
The two decisions also defeat an overly broad version of the argument advanced here. The Roberts Court does not carry institutions while invariably abandoning persons. In these cases, the Court protected the person through a concrete legal route. It identified where the claim must be heard, who must decide the relevant facts, and what legal force the agency’s earlier work may possess.
Questions remain. Formal access to a court does not eliminate the burdens of delay, cost, settlement pressure, or unequal resources. The line between permissible administrative work and unconstitutional adjudication will not answer every problem involving taxes, self-regulatory bodies, or public-private enforcement arrangements.
But those limits do not erase the accomplishment.
The Court preserved the agency’s lawful role while protecting the forum through which the person could answer the government’s accusation. It did not require one institution to perform every function. It identified where final legal consequence had to occur.
The Federal Reserve provides a different comparison.
The Court’s June 29, 2026 Federal Reserve Governor removal decision, No. 25A312, 609 U.S. ___, arose on an application to stay a preliminary injunction, not on final merits review.  Within that posture, however, the internal legal route became concrete. The Court denied the stay after concluding that the government had not shown likely success on arguments that cause was unreviewable, effectively reducible to at-will employment, or incapable of supporting interim preservation of the office. It also concluded, on statutory grounds, that a Governor was entitled to notice and some opportunity to respond before termination.
The opinion tied that treatment to a distinctive combination of statute, central-bank history, monetary function, institutional form, and continuity. Whether another institution could claim comparable treatment remains uncertain.
None of this guarantees that the Governor will remain in office, and the Court did not finally decide whether the asserted cause was factually sufficient. It does something constitutionally prior: it keeps the protection contestable through law while the dispute remains live.
The comparison matters even if the Federal Reserve ultimately proves unique. The decision shows that the Court can connect cause to notice, response, review, and interim preservation rather than allowing the practical result to become final before law can speak.
The modern removal cases supply one more, narrower success.
The Court has stated an increasingly clear constitutional rule for ordinary regulatory and enforcement institutions. Officers exercising substantial executive authority generally must remain sufficiently subject to presidential supervision and removal for the President’s constitutional responsibility to be real. Expertise, multimember design, fixed terms, partisan balance, and the inherited labels “quasi-legislative” and “quasi-judicial” do not, by themselves, place an institution outside that rule.
That clarity performs genuine work.
Congress receives meaningful guidance about the structures it may create. Lower courts can identify the ordinary executive-power center. Agencies know that familiar design features will not necessarily preserve protected tenure. One may dispute the Court’s constitutional reasoning or the breadth of its historical account, but the central rule is no longer hidden.
The later problem is not that removal doctrine contains no law.
It is what that law provides to the officer or claimant who asks what consequence follows from the unconstitutional structure.
That distinction will matter shortly. For now, the comparisons establish the essential point.
The Court can identify a constitutional defect and supply a matched institutional correction.
It can preserve a forum through which the person may appear and defend.
It can give a statutory protection procedural force.
It can state a constitutional rule clearly enough for the legal system to reorganize around it.
These decisions do not weaken the workmanship critique.
They make it possible.
They show that the Court’s incomplete routes cannot all be dismissed as the unavoidable product of deciding constitutional questions one case at a time. They establish that the Court possesses the ability to distinguish functions, preserve useful institutional work, and provide enough law for others to proceed.
They also reveal where that work is easiest.
The successful cases tend to present a visible institutional problem with a relatively direct correction. Final executive authority can be placed in a superior officer. A punitive claim can be routed to a court and jury. A cause protection can be administered through notice and review. A tenure restriction can be severed to restore presidential removal.
The legal system can identify the relationship that changed and the institution responsible for carrying the new rule.
The harder cases require something different.
They involve conduct that is official in one respect and private or electoral in another. They require evidence that crosses the boundary of a constitutional protection. They ask what retrospective consequence follows from a structural violation when the official was properly appointed and legally authorized to act. They depend upon records, timing, causation, and procedures distributed across several institutions. Their development may rely on a single criminal prosecution, one removed officer, or one claimant with the resources and standing to keep the controversy alive.
In those settings, the constitutional rule cannot be completed by moving one box on an institutional chart.
Someone must classify mixed conduct.
Someone must preserve and examine evidence.
Someone must determine what happened under the former structure.
Someone must decide whether the recognized violation produced an injury for which law can provide relief.
And someone must do that work while the evidence remains available, the controversy remains alive, and the answer can still matter.
Institutional litigants and repeat players are often well positioned to sustain that process. They possess continuing existence, records, counsel, and recurring exposure to the legal question. They may return through several cases until the doctrine develops a stable boundary.
The individual person may have only one case.
The term of office expires. The prosecution ends. The claim becomes moot. The evidence disappears. The cost of litigation becomes unsustainable. The person settles, dies, loses standing, or reaches the end of the available procedure before the constitutional category becomes clear.
That difference helps explain why institutional rules may mature more readily than the legal routes needed by the people already governed by them.
It does not justify the difference.
The constitutional question is not whether every difficult case should receive a simple answer. It is who bears the uncertainty while the answer remains difficult.
The burden is visible in the life of the case. Institutions and repeat players often survive long enough for doctrine to mature. A person may have one prosecution, one term of office, one claim, and one body of evidence. The term expires. The prosecution ends. The claim becomes moot. The record disappears. Later cases may clarify the law, but they cannot recreate what the vanished vehicle never carried.
That is why institutional survival is not the final measure of workmanship. Public authority acts through offices and agencies, but it acts upon people. The Court’s successful repairs show that it can preserve a route fitted to the constitutional burden. Presidential immunity tests whether it did so when official and unofficial conduct, evidence, and criminal process were intertwined.
IV. Presidential Immunity — Protecting the Office and Thinning the Legal Record
The constitutional case for presidential immunity begins with a real danger.
The Presidency is not an ordinary public office, and criminal prosecution is not an ordinary form of institutional review. A President must make decisions involving law enforcement, appointments, removal, military command, foreign affairs, national security, interbranch conflict, and the execution of federal law. Those decisions may generate fierce political opposition. Some may later be judged mistaken, abusive, or unlawful. But a constitutional system that permits every contested exercise of presidential authority to become the basis of a successor administration’s criminal case risks changing the office while the President is still performing it.
The prospect of later prosecution may deter action where constitutional decisiveness is required. It may permit prosecutors, judges, and juries to examine presidential functions that Article II entrusts to the office. It may encourage one administration to use criminal process against another. And because criminal proceedings impose burdens before any final judgment—investigation, compulsory process, public accusation, evidentiary exposure, trial—the threat may distort presidential conduct even where the former President would ultimately prevail.
The Court was right to take that danger seriously.
In its 2024 presidential-immunity decision, 603 U.S. 593 (2024), the Court did not declare that everything a President does is beyond criminal law. It instead divided presidential conduct into three broad categories. 
Acts within the President’s conclusive and preclusive constitutional authority receive absolute immunity from criminal prosecution. Other official acts receive at least presumptive immunity. Unofficial conduct receives no immunity merely because the person who performed it was President.
That structure supplies more than a personal privilege.
It protects a constitutional function.
A President cannot be prosecuted for exercising an authority that the Constitution assigns exclusively to the office simply because Congress has written a generally applicable criminal prohibition broad enough to reach the conduct. Where the Constitution commits the decision to the President alone, ordinary criminal law cannot be used to supervise the substance of that decision after the fact.
The Court treated presidential supervision of federal investigation and prosecution as lying near that protected center. Whatever political, ethical, or factual questions might surround a President’s communications with Justice Department officials, the criminal law may not regulate the President’s exercise of exclusive executive authority by converting that exercise itself into an offense.
The Court also recognized that immunity must be resolved early.
If the protection exists to prevent criminal process from intruding upon presidential function, it cannot perform that work only after indictment, discovery, trial, and appeal. A former President asserting immunity must receive a judicial determination before ordinary prosecution proceeds far enough to impose the burdens the immunity is designed to prevent.
That changes the sequence of criminal adjudication.
A court must first determine what kind of presidential conduct is alleged. It must identify whether the conduct falls within exclusive constitutional authority, another official function, or the former President’s private and unofficial activity. Only after that classification can the court know which allegations and evidence remain available to criminal process.
This preliminary gate strongly carries the former President as a constitutional person.
The accused receives more than a defense to liability at the end of trial. The accused receives a right not to be prosecuted for protected official conduct and an early procedure through which that protection can be enforced.
That is real constitutional carry.
The difficulty does not arise because the Court protected the former President too clearly. It arises because the acts of an actual President do not always arrive in the separated categories the doctrine describes.
The President acts at once as constitutional officer, political leader, party figure, candidate, public communicator, and private individual. A conversation may concern federal law and electoral politics. A public statement may speak in the voice of the office while also seeking partisan advantage. A communication with a state official may involve federal responsibilities, state authority, political pressure, and private electoral interests. A course of conduct may move among executive officers, campaign personnel, private attorneys, party organizations, state actors, and members of the public.
The constitutional categories are distinct.
The conduct may not be.
That does not make classification impossible. Courts routinely separate legal relations embedded within one course of events. They distinguish public from private action, protected expression from punishable conduct, lawful purpose from unlawful agreement, and jurisdictional fact from the merits of a claim.
But the more powerful the protection, the more important the classification method becomes.
A court applying presidential immunity must know what makes an act official. The officeholder’s identity cannot be enough, or the immunity would become personal rather than functional. The presence of political motive cannot be enough to make an act unofficial, because Presidents routinely consider politics while exercising legitimate authority. Nor can the alleged illegality of the act itself determine the category, because that would allow prosecutors to defeat immunity simply by describing protected conduct as criminal.
The Court therefore directed lower courts toward an objective assessment of the act’s content, form, and context. It rejected motive and alleged illegality as automatic shortcuts. It required the alleged actions to be examined separately rather than treated as one undifferentiated episode.
Those instructions are important.
They preserve the possibility that a former President may be prosecuted for private conduct while ensuring that the government cannot evade immunity through pleading. They also recognize that a criminal allegation cannot define the constitutional status of the conduct merely by asserting that the President acted for an improper purpose.
But the guidance remains easiest to apply at the poles.
A formal exercise of an exclusive constitutional authority sits near the protected center.
Purely private conduct sits outside it.
The harder cases involve actions that use public office, official relationships, governmental information, or presidential communication in connection with an allegedly private or electoral objective. In those cases, content, form, and context may point in several directions at once.
Consider a presidential communication with an executive officer. The relationship is official. The subject may touch a constitutional responsibility. Yet the alleged purpose may include advancing a private electoral plan.
Or consider a public presidential statement. Speaking to the nation is an ordinary office-function. But the same speech may contain campaign advocacy, factual claims relevant to an alleged private agreement, or directions to political allies acting outside government.
The legal system must determine not only whether the act was official in some general sense, but what follows when official function and allegedly unofficial conduct participate in the same factual sequence.
The Court’s three categories do not themselves answer that problem.
A mixed course of conduct cannot always be separated by assigning one label to each communication and then removing the protected communications from view. The acts may acquire legal meaning through their relationship to one another. An official communication may establish what a private participant knew. A protected meeting may explain the timing or purpose of a later unofficial act. A public exercise of authority may supply context necessary to understand whether an alleged private agreement existed at all.
The Court could not reasonably decide every mixed application in the first presidential-immunity case. The allegations were numerous, the procedural record was incomplete, and initial classification properly belonged to the trial court.
But mixed conduct was not a speculative future complication.
It was already inside the case.
That placed unusual weight on the Court’s account of evidence.
If immunity prohibited only charging or convicting the former President for protected official acts, a court might still preserve the factual sequence while ensuring that the jury did not treat the official acts themselves as criminal. The protected conduct could not form the basis of liability, but it might remain available for some limited evidentiary purpose—context, knowledge, sequence, or the relationship among unprotected acts.
The Court rejected that simple separation.
Its immunity rule reaches beyond the formal charge. Protected official conduct generally may not be used as evidence to prove criminal liability for unofficial acts where that use would subject the presidential function to the burdens and distortions the immunity is meant to prevent.
The Court’s concern is understandable.
An immunity from prosecution would provide little protection if the government could describe the indictment as involving only unofficial conduct, then place extensive evidence of immune presidential action before the jury to prove intent, knowledge, agreement, or motive. The official conduct could become the practical basis for conviction even if it did not appear as the charged offense.
A jury instructed not to punish an official act might still be influenced by it.
A prosecutor might formally comply with the immunity while using the protected exercise of presidential authority to build the narrative of guilt.
An anti-circumvention rule is therefore necessary.
But once immunity becomes a rule about evidence, the constitutional burden changes.
The question is no longer only whether a former President may be held criminally liable for an official act. It becomes what the legal system may know, present, and test when prosecuting an unofficial act that occurred within the same presidential course of conduct.
Evidence does not perform the same function as liability.
The fact that an act cannot lawfully be punished does not always mean that the act is irrelevant to another allegation. A protected communication may establish sequence without establishing guilt. A lawful official action may explain the reaction of a private participant. Public records may show that an event occurred without exposing confidential presidential deliberation. A witness may identify the existence of a meeting without being asked to judge the lawfulness of the President’s official decision.
The doctrine must therefore distinguish several different forms of use.
There is using official conduct as the crime itself.
There is using it as proof that the President had a criminal disposition.
There is using it to infer intent or agreement.
There is using it to establish context, sequence, notice, or the conduct of another participant.
There is introducing a public record of an official act without probing the President’s deliberative process.
Those uses may raise different constitutional risks.
Treating them as identical gives the office powerful protection, but it may also remove the factual relationships through which the remaining unofficial conduct can be understood.
The problem is especially acute where the government alleges cooperation between official and private actors.
An official communication may be protected from criminal scrutiny as an exercise of presidential function. The private participant is not necessarily protected. Yet proving what the private participant agreed to do, understood, or intended may require showing the communication through which the alleged agreement took form.
If the official side of the interaction cannot be presented, the private side may become legally unintelligible.
The same difficulty may affect the former President’s defense.
Official context might show that an allegedly suspicious private act had an innocent governmental explanation. Removing the official setting may leave the jury with a distorted fragment rather than a neutral remainder.
The evidentiary boundary therefore protects both a constitutional office and the fairness of the resulting criminal process.
A workable rule must prevent the prosecution from using protected presidential function as a disguised basis for punishment. It must also permit the court to construct an intelligible case from whatever unprotected conduct remains. That requires attention to the purpose for which evidence is offered, the degree to which it probes protected presidential judgment, the availability of less intrusive proof, and the instructions or procedures capable of preserving the constitutional distinction.
The Court established the protective principle more clearly than it established that method.
What counts as impermissibly probing an official act?
May a witness identify that an official communication occurred without describing protected deliberation?
May a public document establish chronology or context?
May evidence created by a private participant be used when it records or responds to protected presidential conduct?
Can a limiting instruction distinguish lawful context from an unlawful basis for liability?
When official and unofficial actors participate in one alleged plan, how can the government prove the unofficial agreement without presenting the official interaction through which the plan allegedly operated?
These are not merely technical questions for evidentiary specialists.
They determine whether the Court’s statement that unofficial conduct remains prosecutable is legally effective in the cases where unofficial conduct is intertwined with presidential action.
A boundary may remain open in doctrine while becoming nearly impossible to reach through proof.
The Court’s rule thus creates an unusual constitutional gate.
Before a jury can hear the case, the judge must classify the alleged acts, determine which are protected, decide which evidence impermissibly probes those acts, and reconstruct a lawful narrative from what survives. The judge is not simply excluding a discrete privileged communication. The judge may be redrawing the factual structure of the alleged offense.
That preliminary work may be necessary to protect the Presidency.
It also determines what the jury will be permitted to understand.
The jury receives neither the indictment as written nor the historical episode in full. It receives the remainder produced by constitutional classification.
The quality of that remainder matters.
If too much protected material is admitted, the immunity may become hollow. If too much context is removed, the prosecution may become impossible or the surviving allegations may become misleading. The constitutional system must preserve the office without manufacturing an artificial criminal record.
That is the point at which presidential immunity becomes more than a rule protecting a defendant from a charge.
It becomes a rule governing the legal memory of presidential conduct.
The official act may have occurred publicly. It may be recorded in government documents, witness accounts, or communications with private persons. Yet its constitutional protection may prevent it from entering the adjudicatory process through which the allegedly unofficial conduct would otherwise be examined.
The event remains part of history.
Its legal use becomes restricted.
That restriction may be constitutionally justified. But it creates work that the declaration of immunity does not itself perform. Courts must determine how much of the event can remain visible, for what purpose, through which evidence, and with what protection against misuse.
The Court sent that work back to the lower court.
The remand was not a weakness by itself. Trial courts classify evidence and construct records. They possess familiar tools—motions in limine, in-camera review, redaction, severance, and limiting instructions—for doing that work. But those tools provide procedures for applying a constitutional boundary; they do not themselves define which uses of mixed official and unofficial evidence the boundary permits.
The allegations required fact-sensitive judgment. A constitutional rule of this kind could not mature solely through an appellate description of the indictment.
The greater problem emerged from what happened next.
The rule survived.
The case that was expected to give the rule its first full application did not.
The federal prosecution ended before the lower court completed the work the Supreme Court had assigned to it.
No full act-by-act classification followed the remand. No trial court constructed the evidentiary remainder. No jury received a case shaped by the new immunity rule. No final appellate decision explained which official acts could remain visible as context, which evidence impermissibly probed protected conduct, or how an unofficial allegation could proceed when its factual meaning depended upon official presidential action.
The doctrine remained binding.
Its first and most important vehicle disappeared.
That disappearance cannot fairly be attributed to the Supreme Court alone. The litigation unfolded amid an election, a change in the defendant’s constitutional status, executive policy concerning criminal proceedings against a sitting President, and the ordinary delays of a prosecution raising unprecedented legal questions. Several institutions and events contributed to the case’s end. 
The disappearance does not prove that the doctrine was unworkable. It means that the application expected to demonstrate how the doctrine could work against the originating allegations never occurred. 
But the resulting condition still matters.
The Court created a constitutional gate whose hardest applications were left to remand. The remand was expected to show how the distinction between official and unofficial conduct would operate against the actual allegations, how the evidentiary protection would alter the case, and whether a coherent prosecution could survive. When the vehicle ended, the rule entered constitutional law without that application.
Future courts now possess the categories.
They do not possess the worked example.
They know that exclusive presidential authority receives absolute protection, that other official acts receive at least presumptive protection, and that unofficial conduct remains subject to prosecution. They know that motive and alleged illegality cannot by themselves convert an official act into an unofficial one. They know that immune official conduct cannot be used in a manner that defeats the constitutional protection.
What they do not yet know with comparable clarity is how those propositions fit together when one alleged course of conduct crosses all three categories.
That absence is not merely scholarly inconvenience.
The doctrine governs before the next comparable case arrives.
Prosecutors must decide what may be investigated and alleged. Grand juries must be presented with indictments capable of surviving constitutional screening. Trial judges must determine what information they may examine in classifying conduct without allowing the classification inquiry itself to become an intrusion into protected presidential function. Witnesses may not know which portions of their interactions remain legally available. Future Presidents must know what office-function is protected. Future former Presidents must know where the protected function ends. Private actors who communicate with the President must know whether and how their own conduct can be proved when the presidential side of the interaction is constitutionally screened.
The Court need not answer each of those questions in advance.
But the legal system must answer them somewhere.
The disappearance of the original prosecution makes that “somewhere” uncertain. A future case would require another extraordinary conjunction: alleged criminal conduct involving a former President, a viable prosecution, a surviving defendant, evidence capable of reaching the classification stage, enough time for lower-court development, and a controversy that remains alive through appellate review.
That may occur.
It is not guaranteed to occur.
A future case is therefore not a present legal route merely because one can be imagined. It cannot classify the existing allegations, preserve the original record, or complete the adjudication that ended. It may involve different powers, different evidence, different private actors, and a different mixture of official and unofficial conduct.
The law can develop through later cases.
It cannot retroactively make the vanished case carry the work it never performed.
This is where vehicle survival becomes constitutionally consequential.
Institutions frequently survive long enough to obtain clarification. Agencies remain in existence. Regulated industries generate recurring disputes. Congress can legislate again. Executive officers continue to exercise similar powers. A doctrine concerning removal, agency adjudication, or superior review may return through several cases until its boundary becomes clearer.
A criminal prosecution of one former President is not that kind of vehicle.
It is contingent upon one defendant, one body of evidence, one prosecutor, one period of constitutional status, and one sequence of political and legal events. When it ends, the legal system cannot simply substitute a functionally identical case from an institutional inventory.
That difference helps explain why the Court’s institutional doctrines may mature while the person-facing and evidentiary routes remain thin.
It does not eliminate the Court’s responsibility for the rule it released.
The former President was carried through the transition with considerable strength.
The doctrine provided an early constitutional hearing. It prevented ordinary criminal process from proceeding until the alleged conduct had been classified. It barred prosecution for conduct falling within exclusive presidential authority. It presumptively protected other official acts. It limited evidentiary uses capable of circumventing that protection. And because the protection operates before trial, it guarded against the process itself becoming the constitutional injury.
Those are not merely institutional benefits to “the Presidency” as an abstraction.
They are legal protections held by an actual defendant.
A human being facing the coercive force of criminal law received a route through which the government’s authority could be challenged before the burdens of prosecution fully attached.
That fact must remain visible.
The human center of constitutional law cannot mean that the person accused of crime disappears whenever the accusation carries public importance. Nor can public demand for accountability erase the constitutional interests of the officeholder. The former President is not merely an institution wearing a human face. The former President is a defendant entitled to every protection the Constitution lawfully supplies.
But the former President is not the only human relation inside the case.
Criminal process also involves witnesses asked to place events into a legal record, jurors asked to determine facts, private participants whose conduct may be distinct from the President’s, and persons affected by the events alleged. It involves future defendants and prosecutors who must understand the boundary of the rule. It involves a public legal order whose interest is not a guaranteed conviction, but a lawful means of determining whether unofficial criminal conduct occurred.
Those interests do not all have equal constitutional status.
They do not all require the prosecution to proceed.
A person affected by alleged presidential conduct does not possess a personal right to criminal punishment. A prosecutor does not possess a constitutional entitlement to win. The public cannot demand that protected official action be exposed merely because the events are important. The jury has no authority to hear evidence the Constitution removes from criminal adjudication.
The question is narrower.
After the Court protects the office and the defendant, does the legal system retain a workable means of examining the unofficial conduct that the Court says remains outside the immunity?
That is the relation the disappeared case was expected to test.
Criminal adjudication ordinarily performs several functions at once. It permits the government to state an accusation. It requires the government to prove the charge under a demanding legal standard. It permits the accused to contest the evidence, confront witnesses, offer an alternative account, and obtain acquittal if the proof fails. It gives a judge authority to control the proceeding and a jury authority to determine contested facts. It produces a record in which the legal system explains why liability did or did not follow.
A trial does not guarantee truth.
It does not guarantee justice.
It does not guarantee public agreement.
But it is an authorized process through which accusation becomes either established legal consequence or failure of proof.
When presidential immunity removes conduct and evidence from that process, the displaced functions do not cease to matter.
The protection may be constitutionally necessary. Yet the legal system must still determine whether any unofficial allegation can be understood and tried without the protected material. It must preserve enough of the factual sequence to avoid transforming the remaining case into fiction. It must protect the defendant from punishment based on immune conduct while protecting the integrity of adjudication against a record so fragmented that neither prosecution nor defense can present an intelligible account.
That is exacting work.
The Court’s opinion gave judges constitutional authority to perform it.
It gave them less guidance about how.
A lower court must still distinguish the classification of conduct from the use of evidence. An act may be immune from prosecution while a public record, a private response, or neutral chronology remains relevant to an unofficial allegation. Other uses may probe protected presidential judgment or invite punishment through context. Without a method for separating those functions, caution predictably favors exclusion, and the unofficial boundary may become narrower in practice than it appears in doctrine.
The jury cannot repair that problem. Immunity must be resolved before trial, so the judge decides what factual world the jury may enter. The lawful remainder must protect official function without rendering the unprotected conduct unintelligible or misleading.
The Court established both demands but not their relation. Because the remand ended, the legal system never saw whether they could be made to coexist in the case that produced the rule.
Other courts have since begun applying or assessing the doctrine in different settings, including disputes over whether particular evidence reflected official acts. Those applications matter. They do not, however, supply the full act-by-act, evidentiary, and trial application that the remand in the originating federal prosecution was expected to produce. 
That loss reaches beyond prosecution and verdict.
It reaches the legal record.
A public event and a legal event are not the same thing. Presidential conduct may be extensively reported, recorded, investigated, discussed, and preserved in archives. The public may form confident judgments about it. Historians may later reconstruct the episode in great detail.
None of that is insignificant.
But a public account does not automatically become a legal finding. Journalism does not ordinarily compel testimony under the rules governing criminal process. Historical judgment does not apply burdens of proof. Political argument does not provide confrontation or cross-examination. An archive may preserve evidence without determining what the evidence proves under law.
The criminal record would have performed a different function.
It would have shown which allegations remained legally available after immunity, which evidence could be tested, which facts the government could prove, which account the defendant could offer, and whether the surviving unofficial conduct satisfied the elements of an offense.
That record could have ended in conviction.
It could have ended in acquittal.
It could have ended in dismissal because the lawful evidence was insufficient.
Any of those outcomes would have been a legal answer.
The constitutional deficiency is not that the case failed to produce a preferred result. It is that the new rule helped determine which legal answer was possible while the process expected to develop and apply the rule never reached completion.
The public may still remember the events.
The legal system remembers them differently.
It remembers the Supreme Court’s constitutional categories. It remembers the existence of an indictment and the procedural history of the case. It remembers that official and unofficial conduct must be separated and that protected official conduct cannot be used to defeat immunity.
It does not possess a completed adjudicatory record showing how those commands applied to the alleged conduct.
That gap will matter whenever later actors invoke the case as evidence of either criminal wrongdoing or constitutional exoneration.
The ended prosecution supplied neither.
The absence of a trial is not proof that the allegations were true. It is not proof that they were false. Immunity is not innocence. Dismissal without adjudication is not conviction. Political victory is not a legal judgment. Historical condemnation is not a verdict.
A constitutional order should be capable of preserving those distinctions.
Otherwise the legal vacuum becomes available for competing forms of public certainty that the law itself never authorized.
Political, congressional, civil, state, journalistic, and historical routes may preserve portions of the public answer. None automatically supplies the criminal adjudication that ended, and the Court’s opinion did not coordinate those different forms of answer into a substitute for it. 
The Court gave the protected side of the constitutional boundary a strong legal route.
The former President could invoke immunity early. The court had to classify the conduct. Exclusive authority received categorical protection. Other official conduct received a presumption. The evidentiary rule prevented criminal process from using protected action as a disguised path to liability.
The unprotected side received a declaration:
Unofficial conduct remains prosecutable.
But where official and unofficial actions were intertwined, the opinion supplied less guidance about how the unofficial conduct could be proved without using the protected context, how the jury could receive an intelligible record, and how the public legal order could preserve an authorized answer if the prosecution did not survive.
The difference is not that one side received every desired outcome and the other did not.
The difference is structural.
Office protection received a constitutional gate, an early hearing, a burden placed upon the government, appellate protection, and an evidentiary shield.
The examination of unprotected conduct remained dependent upon a difficult classification process, an uncertain evidentiary remainder, a fragile prosecution, and future cases that may never reproduce the same constitutional problem.
The Court carried the Presidency as an office.
It carried the former President as a defendant.
It did not carry with equal strength the legal system’s capacity to examine unofficial conduct entangled with presidential action.
That does not make the immunity illegitimate.
It makes the reset incomplete at the point where protection had to coexist with proof.
The Court identified a genuine constitutional danger: criminal process can distort presidential function.
It answered that danger forcefully.
It gave a weaker answer to the reciprocal danger: official-function protection can distort or disable the legal examination of unofficial crime.
A constitutional order capable of protecting the Presidency must also remain capable of distinguishing protection from impunity, immunity from innocence, and political history from adjudicated law.
The presidential-immunity decision states those distinctions at the level of doctrine. 
It does not yet make them equally available in practice.
That is why presidential immunity is the clearest site of Apex Failure in the Court’s executive-power architecture.
The failure is not that the Court protected the office.
The failure is that the office received the strongest route while the evidentiary, adjudicatory, and public-record work displaced by that protection was left to a case that vanished and to institutions whose separate forms of answer were never made to fit together.
The rule survived.
The adjudication did not.
V. Removal — Structural Repair and Remedial Consequence
The Court may agree that the constitutional structure was unlawful and still leave the person who bore it without meaningful consequence.
That is the removal-remedy problem in human terms.
The tenure restriction may be invalidated. Presidential supervisory authority may be restored. The offending statutory provision may be severed. The agency may continue operating under the corrected arrangement. The constitutional rule may become clearer for Congress and future officers.
And the officer, regulated party, shareholder, or other claimant who brought the defect before the courts may still receive no reinstatement, no vacatur, no backward-looking relief, and no practical answer to what happened while the unconstitutional structure remained in place.
Removal therefore provides the necessary companion to presidential immunity.
Immunity arose in an extraordinary setting. It required the Court to construct a new constitutional protection for the Presidency, distinguish official from unofficial conduct, regulate the use of evidence, and protect the office against the burdens of criminal process. A defender of the decision can reasonably say that difficult boundaries were inevitable because the problem itself was unprecedented.
Removal is different.
The Roberts Court has developed its modern removal doctrine across multiple cases, institutions, and remedial disputes. The governing rule has matured by stages. Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Board, 561 U.S. 477 (2010), rejected two successive layers of for-cause protection between the President and officers exercising substantial federal authority. Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, 591 U.S. 197 (2020), invalidated a single protected Director exercising significant executive power. Collins v. Yellen, 594 U.S. 220 (2021), extended that reasoning to the Federal Housing Finance Agency. The Court’s later treatment of the Federal Trade Commission placed that body’s modern regulatory, enforcement, administrative, and litigation functions within the ordinary executive-power rule. 
Whatever disagreement remains about history, exceptions, or the precise constitutional scope of presidential control, the institutional center is now substantially clear.
Officers who direct ordinary executive administration generally must remain sufficiently subject to presidential supervision and removal for presidential responsibility to be real. Expertise, fixed terms, multimember design, political balance, or inherited descriptions of an agency as “quasi-legislative” or “quasi-judicial” do not, standing alone, remove an institution from that principle.
That rule performs important constitutional work.
It tells Congress that statutory design cannot place substantial executive authority beyond a constitutionally sufficient line of presidential control. It tells lower courts which common agency features no longer preserve the older FTC model. It tells Presidents that responsibility for execution is accompanied by authority to direct and replace the officers through whom execution occurs. It gives the public a clearer location for political responsibility.
The rule may be contested.
It is not empty.
The workmanship problem begins where that institutional clarity meets the person who must live under it.
Removal takes place through a human officeholder.
A named person is told to leave an office, is threatened with removal, or is forced to litigate whether Congress lawfully conditioned removal upon cause. The constitutional dispute may concern presidential authority and institutional design, but its immediate consequence is carried by someone whose service, duties, authority, salary, reputation, and limited term of office are placed at risk.
The 2026 FTC Commissioner removal decision, No. 25-332, 609 U.S. ___ (June 29, 2026), makes that sequence concrete. A commissioner removed without any of the statutory cause grounds sued for restoration. The district court declared the removal unlawful and enjoined interference with her duties. The Supreme Court stayed that order, granted review before judgment, and ultimately held the tenure protection unconstitutional. A second commissioner who had also sought restoration resigned during the litigation, and his claim became moot.
This is not an example of an officer ultimately entitled to the lost service; the Court rejected the tenure protection. It is an example of how restoration, interim service, mootness, and final constitutional classification can separate while the litigation runs.
That does not give the officer a constitutional right to remain merely because Congress enacted a tenure restriction.
If the restriction itself violates Article II, judicial process cannot convert the invalid condition into a lawful entitlement. An officer exercising ordinary executive authority cannot demand reinstatement on the sole ground that a statute attempted to prevent the President from exercising constitutionally required control.
But the absence of a right to continued tenure does not eliminate every person-facing question.
When did the removal become legally effective?
What notice was required?
Did the President invoke a ground recognized by the governing statute?
Was the officeholder entitled to respond?
Could a court preserve the office while the legality of the removal was tested?
What became of decisions made while the person’s status remained disputed?
Could a later judgment provide any meaningful relief after the statutory term had expired or the office had been filled by someone else?
The answers depend heavily upon the Court’s classification of the office.
Where an agency falls within the ordinary executive-power core, presidential removal receives constitutional priority. The invalid tenure restriction cannot keep the officer in place. The agency continues under leadership answerable through the corrected chain of command.
That may be the proper constitutional result.
But it can make the institutional answer much clearer than the officer’s remaining legal position.
The President obtains control.
The protected-tenure relation ends.
The government moves forward.
The officer may be left to litigate questions of salary, effective date, declaratory relief, or other residual consequence after the service itself can no longer be restored.
Timing is therefore not incidental to removal remedy.
An interim order may formally preserve the merits while practically determining who exercises the office during the period that matters. If removal takes effect immediately, the replacement officer assumes authority, the institution reorganizes, official decisions continue, and the disputed term runs down. Even if the former officer later prevails on a narrow statutory or procedural issue, the original period of service may be impossible to recover.
The litigation remains alive.
The constitutional interest may not.
That does not mean interim relief must always favor the officer. Presidential control may itself suffer ongoing constitutional injury if a person whom the President has lawfully removed continues exercising executive authority during years of litigation. Courts must weigh real harms on both sides.
The point is that a later merits judgment cannot always repair the consequence of an earlier practical decision.
Law may eventually identify who was right.
It cannot return the time during which the office was held by someone else.
The Federal Reserve stay decision shows the importance of timing. There, notice, response, judicial review, and interim preservation kept the statutory tenure protection legally operative while its application was litigated. That treatment does not travel automatically to every office, but it shows how process can preserve an officeholder’s claim before the disputed term is lost.  
Where the Court invalidates the tenure restriction, the institutional correction is necessarily different. The President cannot be required to satisfy a cause standard that Congress had no constitutional authority to impose. Reinstatement cannot preserve an unlawful arrangement. Immediate restoration of presidential control may be the very remedy the Constitution requires.
Yet the contrast remains revealing.
One office receives cause, notice, response, review, and possible interim preservation because its institutional relation survives constitutional scrutiny.
Another receives a clear rule that the President may remove, while the law gives much less attention to what remains of the individual officeholder’s claim once the constitutional classification has been made.
That difference may be legally justified.
It is still a difference in what the constitutional architecture carries.
The removal-remedy problem becomes more difficult when the challenger is not the removed officer.
A regulated party may contest an agency action on the ground that the official who exercised authority was unconstitutionally insulated from presidential removal. Shareholders may challenge a financial arrangement executed by an agency headed under an invalid tenure provision. A company may seek to resist an investigation, adjudication, or enforcement action produced through the defective structure.
The person here may be a corporation, association, shareholder group, or another legal claimant rather than a literal individual removed from office.
That register difference matters.
A shareholder seeking financial relief is not situated like an officer who has lost a public position. A corporation does not bear public action in the same human sense as a named person ordered from an office. The claims, injuries, remedies, and legal interests differ.
But the remedial question exposes the same constitutional gap.
The Court has held the governing structure unlawful.
What does that recognition carry to the party governed by action taken under it?
Collins v. Yellen supplies the central answer.
The Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency was properly appointed. The office itself possessed statutory authority. The constitutional defect lay in Congress’s restriction upon the President’s ability to remove the Director, not in the Director’s initial legal entitlement to hold the office.
That distinction prevented the violation from automatically invalidating every action the agency had taken.
The result has substantial constitutional and practical force.
An appointment defect may mean that the person exercising authority had no lawful right to occupy the office at all. A removal defect is different. The officer may have been properly appointed, may have possessed all statutory powers of the office, and may have taken action the President supported. The unconstitutional tenure protection may never have changed the particular decision under challenge.
Treating every agency action as void would impose extraordinary disruption without proving any connection between the defective structure and the action affecting the claimant.
Years of administration could be reopened.
Settled transactions could become uncertain.
Regulated parties might receive windfalls unrelated to the constitutional wrong.
An agency whose leader was properly appointed could lose the legal effect of its entire work because of one statutory condition governing possible presidential removal.
The Court therefore refused automatic invalidation.
A claimant seeking retrospective relief must show that the unlawful removal restriction caused legally cognizable harm connected to the challenged action.
That rule is defensible.
It is also where the asymmetry becomes visible.
At the structural level, the Court’s judgment is categorical.
Congress imposed an unconstitutional restriction upon presidential removal.
The restriction impaired the constitutionally required relation between executive authority and presidential control.
The statute must be corrected.
At the remedial level, the answer becomes conditional.
Did the restriction actually affect the government action challenged by this claimant?
Would the President have removed the officer?
Did the President oppose the policy or decision?
Would a differently removable official have acted otherwise?
Did the invalid tenure protection prevent presidential correction in a way that produced the claimant’s injury?
Those questions may be appropriate to retrospective relief.
But they place a difficult burden upon the person seeking to connect constitutional structure to lived legal consequence.
The proof may depend on internal executive evidence—or upon a counterfactual record that was never created. A claimant may know that Congress unlawfully protected an agency head and that the agency took action affecting the claimant. But the remedial test can require substantiated evidence of a presidential desire to remove the officer, a perceived inability to do so because of the invalid provision, and a nexus between that frustrated removal and the particular action challenged. Post-Collins decisions have denied relief where that connection was not shown. See Collins v. Department of the Treasury, 83 F.4th 970 (5th Cir. 2023); Kaufmann v. Kijakazi, 32 F.4th 843 (9th Cir. 2022). 
The constitutional violation is concrete.
Its causal effect may be structurally difficult to prove.
This is not unique to removal doctrine. Remedies frequently require proof of injury and causation. Courts properly distinguish a legal violation from the relief available to a particular claimant. The Constitution does not guarantee vacatur, damages, or reversal whenever a litigant identifies an error somewhere in the government’s structure.
The difficulty is the relation between the Court’s theory of violation and its theory of consequence.
The modern removal cases treat presidential control as a substantive constitutional capacity. Removal is not merely a formal personnel option. It permits direction, correction, supervision, and responsibility. A protected officer may resist presidential preferences because the President cannot readily replace them. That insulation may alter administration even where no removal is attempted.
The Court’s constitutional account therefore treats the restriction as capable of shaping government action throughout the period it remains in force.
But when the claimant seeks relief, the violation may be treated as consequential only if the claimant can show a more specific event: a frustrated removal, an identifiable presidential objection, or another concrete link between the invalid restriction and the challenged action.
The theory is broad enough to invalidate the structure.
The remedial proof can be much narrower.
That does not make the causal requirement incoherent.
It makes the person-facing route thin.
The Court restores presidential control by declaring the restriction invalid for the institution as a whole. The person or claimant receives retrospective consequence only by proving that the invalid relation altered a particular action in a manner the available record may not reveal.
The institution is repaired categorically.
The claimant is carried conditionally.
Severability reinforces that distinction.
When the offending removal provision can be separated from the rest of the statute, the agency survives. Its authority remains. The officers continue exercising their assigned functions under the constitutionally corrected removal relation.
That is often sound remedial judgment.
Congress may have preferred the agency with protected tenure, but it may also have preferred an agency subject to presidential removal over no agency at all. Invalidating the whole statutory scheme could destroy lawful and necessary public administration because one portion of the leadership design was unconstitutional.
Severance preserves the institution.
It does not answer every action taken before the correction.
A person may have been investigated, regulated, adjudicated, sanctioned, or financially affected while the unlawful restriction remained in force. The Court can remove the restriction prospectively without determining whether the prior action should stand, be reconsidered, or produce compensation.
That is not a defect in severability itself.
It is evidence that institutional repair and person-facing consequence are different constitutional tasks.
Ratification may also preserve continuity.
An official operating under the corrected arrangement may reconsider and adopt an earlier action. That can give the institution lawful ownership of the decision without forcing the entire administrative process to begin again.
Ratification may be entirely appropriate.
But it answers a particular question:
Does an officer who is now properly accountable possess authority to adopt this action for the institution?
It does not necessarily answer whether the earlier structure injured the claimant, whether the original record was shaped by the protected officer’s independence, whether delay changed the person’s legal position, or whether the action would have been different had presidential control been constitutionally available when the decision was first made.
Ratification can cure present authority.
It cannot automatically cure history.
The same distinction governs prospective relief.
A court may declare the tenure restriction unconstitutional and ensure that future agency action occurs under proper presidential control. That judgment prevents recurrence. It clarifies the law. It may alter the behavior of officers and Presidents even without vacating a prior decision.
Prospective correction matters.
But the person who bore earlier government action may still be left with the following answer:
The structure was unconstitutional.
The institution has now been repaired.
The prior action remains.
No further relief follows unless you can prove that the invalid removal restriction caused this particular result.
That may be the legally correct outcome in a given case.
It is not no outcome.
It means the Constitution has answered primarily at the level of institutional architecture.
The presidential relation has been restored.
The statutory defect has been removed.
The agency continues under the proper chain of control.
The person receives recognition of the violation but no necessary change in legal consequence.
Recognition has value.
It may prevent future harm, guide Congress, alter administration, support declaratory relief, or preserve a constitutional rule that later claimants can invoke.
But recognition does not itself vacate an order, restore an office, return property, reopen an adjudication, correct an official record, or compensate injury.
A structure can be declared unlawful while the acts performed through it remain fully effective against the people who challenged it.
That is the removal-remedy pressure.
The strongest defense is institutional stability.
Government must be able to operate. Officers make thousands of decisions. Agencies administer continuing programs. Regulated parties and members of the public rely upon official acts. The Constitution should not become a mechanism by which one tenure defect erases an entire body of administration unrelated to the President’s actual preferences.
That defense deserves full weight.
But stability cannot silently become the whole constitutional answer.
The law must still distinguish between preserving government and leaving the constitutional violation without any route to consequence. Causation can perform that work only if the claimant has a realistic means of proving it. Ratification can perform it only if reconsideration is genuine rather than formal. Declaratory relief can perform it only if prospective recognition addresses the legal interest that remains. Reinstatement can perform it only while service is still possible and while the tenure protection itself is lawful.
A remedy need not be maximal.
It must be available in a form the claimant can actually reach where the violation produced injury.
Otherwise, the constitutional rule may operate asymmetrically.
The Court can see the removal restriction clearly enough to invalidate it for the purpose of presidential power.
The claimant cannot show the restriction clearly enough to obtain relief from the action taken under it.
That asymmetry is particularly striking because the doctrine’s theory of accountability depends upon consequence.
The Court does not protect presidential removal as an empty symbol. It protects removal because the capacity to replace officers is supposed to affect how administration occurs. It enables supervision. It permits correction. It makes presidential responsibility practical rather than rhetorical.
If removal truly matters in those ways, then an unlawful restriction may matter even where no President issued a formal removal order.
It may matter through incentives, policy independence, institutional resistance, or the anticipated limits of presidential intervention.
Yet those diffuse effects are exactly what a particular claimant may find hardest to prove.
The constitutional doctrine treats control as powerful enough to reshape the institution.
The remedial doctrine may require evidence of a narrower, decision-specific disruption before the person receives relief.
The gap does not prove that automatic vacatur is the right answer.
It proves that the remedy problem cannot be treated as a technical afterthought.
It is where the Court’s institutional theory reaches the person who asks what the theory means in law.
Removal and presidential immunity are not doctrinal twins.
Immunity protects a constitutional office from criminal process. Removal restores presidential supervision over executive administration. One concerns evidence, mixed conduct, prosecution, and adjudicatory record. The other concerns tenure, control, causation, continuity, and remedy.
They pair because the Court’s workmanship becomes uneven at the same constitutional location.
In immunity, the office and defendant receive a strong threshold route. The legal examination of unofficial conduct intertwined with protected action receives a thinner evidentiary and adjudicatory route.
In removal, presidential control and institutional continuity receive a strong structural rule. The officer or claimant seeking retrospective consequence receives a narrower route governed by classification, timing, causation, and proof.
The person is not always situated the same way.
The removed officer is literally displaced from public service. The Collins claimant may be a corporate or shareholder plaintiff challenging government action. Their injuries are different. Their available remedies are different. The constitutional law reaches them through different legal forms.
What they share is the relation between recognized defect and carried consequence.
The Court tells each that the governing structure matters.
The removal restriction was unconstitutional.
The Presidency was denied a control relation the Constitution requires.
The statute must yield.
But when the person asks what that constitutional conclusion changes for them, the answer becomes far more contingent.
Was the office’s statutory protection valid or invalid?
Did review arrive before the term became unrecoverable?
Can the claimant prove what the President would have done?
Can internal executive influence be reconstructed?
Did ratification truly reconsider the action?
Can the prior decision be challenged without destabilizing lawful administration?
Does any remedy remain after prospective correction has repaired the institution?
These are legitimate legal questions.
Their cumulative effect is to place the uncertainty upon the person who already bore public action under the defective structure.
Removal therefore confirms that the problem is not unique to the unprecedented difficulty of presidential immunity. Here the constitutional field is mature and the institutional rule is clear, yet the same asymmetry remains: office, hierarchy, presidential responsibility, and future operation are carried with considerable precision; the person’s path from violation to consequence is not. 
The point is not that every claimant should prevail.
The point is not that every agency action taken under a defective tenure provision should be void.
The point is not that institutional continuity is illegitimate.
The point is that constitutional violation and constitutional consequence can become separated at the exact moment the person seeks to move from one to the other.
Causation, severability, and ratification may each be legally correct. The workmanship question is whether they leave a realistic route for proving injury, distinguish prospective correction from retrospective answer, and prevent institutional continuity from erasing what happened under the defect. 
Apex workmanship does not require the strongest remedy.
It requires a route through which the legal system can distinguish no injury from injury without remedy, lawful continuity from remedial erasure, and correction for the future from an answer to what happened in the past.
The modern removal line gives the institution a strong constitutional answer.
The person often receives a harder question.
That is the companion failure.
The Court may agree that the structure was unconstitutional.
It may repair the structure.
It may preserve the agency.
It may restore presidential control.
And the person who bore the defective arrangement may still receive nothing capable of answering what the violation did.
The institution moves forward.
The consequence remains behind.
VI. What the Other Routes Can—and Cannot—Do
The Supreme Court is not the whole constitutional system.
Congress legislates and investigates. Presidents answer to elections. The House may impeach and the Senate may try federal officers. Agencies revise their procedures. Executive superiors review subordinate decisions. Lower courts apply constitutional rules to new facts. Civil suits may produce discovery and remedy. States exercise their own lawful authority. Journalists, archives, and historians preserve events that courts never adjudicate.
A serious workmanship critique must leave room for that distributed order.
The Court should not attempt to become prosecutor, legislature, agency administrator, trial court, electoral body, and public archive at once. A constitutional decision may properly assign later work to another institution. Indeed, several of the successful repairs examined here depend upon that division. Arthrex works because an executive superior can review the subordinate decision. Jarkesy and AT&T work because the agency and the Article III court perform different parts of the enforcement process. The Federal Reserve route works because statutory process, presidential authority, and judicial review remain distinct but connected.
The existence of another institution, however, does not show that the institution can perform the particular work the Court’s decision has displaced.
Different routes produce different kinds of answer.
Elections and impeachment can impose political consequence. Courts can adjudicate legal claims. Congress and agencies can redesign institutions. Civil proceedings may answer individual injury. Journalism and historical memory can preserve public knowledge.
Those functions may support one another.
They are not interchangeable.
Political judgment
Elections are an essential constitutional route.
They permit the public to select Presidents and legislators, reject an administration’s direction, change executive policy, and place political responsibility upon identifiable officeholders. The Roberts Court’s removal doctrine depends substantially upon that relation. If the President can supervise executive administration, the public can know whom to credit or blame for the way federal law is carried out.
That is real accountability.
It is not adjudication.
An election does not determine whether a particular removal complied with law. It does not decide whether an agency action should be vacated. It does not classify presidential conduct as official or unofficial. It does not determine whether evidence proves the elements of a criminal offense. It cannot reinstate an officer’s lost term or compensate an individual injury.
Voters decide among candidates for countless reasons. They may reject an officeholder who acted lawfully or reelect one who acted unlawfully. They may care more about economic conditions, party allegiance, foreign affairs, personal character, or future policy than about the legal status of one past act.
That breadth is part of democratic judgment.
It is also why political accountability cannot substitute for a legal answer.
Impeachment is more focused, but its constitutional work is still different.
It permits the political branches to address grave abuse of federal office. It can remove an officer, prevent future officeholding, expose misconduct, and create a public constitutional record. For a sitting President, it may provide the only process capable of answering certain dangers without subjecting the office to ordinary criminal control while the President remains in power.
But impeachment concerns the holding and abuse of public office. Criminal adjudication concerns liability under law. The participants, procedures, standards, timing, and possible consequences differ.
A Senate acquittal does not necessarily establish that no crime occurred. It may reflect factual doubt, legal disagreement, political judgment, institutional caution, or the proximity of the officer’s departure. Nor can impeachment restore a removed agency official, vacate an administrative order, or compensate a claimant governed by an unconstitutional structure.
Political judgment may be powerful.
It does not become legal judgment merely because no better route survived.
Institutional correction
Congress and executive institutions possess substantial capacity to respond after the Court changes constitutional law.
Congress can redesign an agency, create a new cause of action, require reporting, alter an enforcement process, preserve records, appropriate funds, or move final adjudication into an Article III court. An agency can revise internal review, separate functions, reconsider an earlier decision, or place final authority in a properly accountable officer. Executive officials can issue new rules and policies under the corrected constitutional arrangement.
Much of constitutional government depends upon that work.
But institutional correction usually operates prospectively.
Congress cannot restore the years an officeholder has already lost. A new procedure does not necessarily reopen the proceeding of the person who endured the old one. An agency’s ratification of its earlier action may cure present authority without resolving whether the earlier structure injured the claimant. A statute enacted after a constitutional reset may improve the next case while arriving too late for the first person governed by the change.
Institutional redesign may also be limited by the decision itself.
Congress cannot reenact the same tenure protection after the Court has held it unconstitutional. It cannot authorize a jury to punish conduct the Constitution places beyond criminal prosecution. An agency cannot grant itself Article III authority or disregard an evidentiary protection imposed by the Supreme Court.
Another institution may possess broad public power and still lack authority over the precise object left behind.
That is why congressional or agency possibility is not enough.
The question is whether the institution can act while an answer still matters, upon the evidence that remains, and with legal authority to provide the kind of consequence the burden requires.
Sometimes it can.
Sometimes the best available constitutional answer is genuinely prospective. A structure is corrected, recurrence is prevented, and no retrospective remedy is legally appropriate.
But that conclusion must be reached through law.
It cannot be assumed merely because the institution has moved on.
Judicial development and individual remedy
Lower courts are indispensable to constitutional development.
They classify conduct, build records, decide remedies, distinguish precedents, and test how Supreme Court rules apply outside the clean facts of the original case. No serious conception of apex workmanship should demand that the Supreme Court perform that work for them.
But a lower court can develop only the law that has been made available to it.
It cannot admit evidence the Supreme Court’s constitutional rule forbids. It cannot create a cause of action that does not exist. It cannot disregard a binding classification. It cannot reconstruct a vanished record or keep a controversy alive after jurisdiction has disappeared.
Lower courts also require cases.
A person must possess standing, resources, counsel, evidence, a viable claim, and enough time to survive the procedure. A criminal prosecution must remain legally and institutionally possible. An officer’s term must not expire before relief becomes meaningless. A remedial dispute must present facts capable of proving the causal connection the doctrine demands.
Future litigation can properly clarify genuine uncertainty.
But future litigation is not a present route for the person whose case has ended.
The future case has no claimant, no evidence, no record, and no guarantee of arrival. It may involve different conduct, a different office, or a different constitutional power. It cannot supply the judgment that the vanished case never produced.
Civil and state proceedings may sometimes carry part of the burden.
A civil action may permit discovery, compensation, injunction, or declaratory relief. A state court may adjudicate conduct within lawful state authority. Those proceedings can preserve evidence and provide a forum where federal criminal process does not.
But they bring their own limits.
The plaintiff must have standing and a valid legal claim. Immunity may apply. Damages may be unavailable. Causation may remain difficult to prove. State authority remains subject to federal supremacy and the constitutional protection of official presidential function. Evidence excluded because of the federal immunity rule may remain unavailable in another forum.
A different court is not automatically a different constitutional answer.
The proceeding must have authority over the conduct, access to the necessary evidence, and power to provide a remedy fitted to the injury.
Where those conditions exist, the route is real.
Where they do not, invoking “civil litigation” or “state process” merely gives the missing answer another name.
Record and memory
Public knowledge may survive even when adjudication does not. Congressional hearings, journalism, archives, scholarship, and historical reconstruction can preserve events that courts never resolve. That work is indispensable, but it does not apply legal burdens of proof, compel testimony through judicial procedure, or produce an authorized judgment.
The distinction matters when unfinished litigation is later treated as proof of either guilt or exoneration. Memory can carry the event. It cannot create the judgment.
The burden must fit the route
None of these alternative routes is false.
Political accountability can redirect government. Impeachment can answer abuse of office. Congress can investigate and redesign. Agencies can repair procedures. Lower courts can develop doctrine. Civil litigation can remedy some injuries. States can exercise lawful authority. Archives and historical judgment can preserve public memory.
The error is to treat their existence as proof that the constitutional work displaced by the Court has been completed.
The relevant question is fit.
Does the proposed route possess authority over the legal object?
Can it obtain and test the evidence?
Can the affected person enter the process?
Can it act before time makes relief meaningless?
Can it produce the relevant form of judgment or remedy?
Can it preserve a record capable of carrying the answer forward?
A route need not perform every public function. It must perform the function assigned to it.
An election may properly answer political direction without deciding criminal guilt. An archive may preserve memory without supplying remedy. A court may dismiss a claim without resolving the whole historical controversy. Congress may correct the next institutional design without reopening every action taken under the old one.
Constitutional coherence does not require one institution to do everything.
It requires the work not to disappear between them.
That is the weakness exposed by presidential immunity and removal remedy.
In immunity, elections, impeachment, congressional investigation, civil process, state authority, and public memory may each preserve part of the answer. None necessarily reconstructs the criminal adjudication of unofficial conduct when protected official action supplies essential context.
In removal, prospective correction, severability, ratification, and political responsibility may preserve the institution. None necessarily gives the officer or claimant a practical way to show what the unconstitutional restriction did in the past.
The Court may lawfully rely upon distributed government after it acts.
But distribution is not completion unless the routes connect.
A route is not adequate because it can be named.
It must carry the burden assigned to it.
VII. Apex Failure
Apex Failure is not a verdict on every decision of the Roberts Court.
It is a judgment about workmanship at a particular constitutional location.
The Court materially changes who may exercise authority, which institution may decide, what process may proceed, or which conduct receives constitutional protection. Its judgment binds immediately. The surrounding legal order must then continue through the arrangement the Court has created.
The Court fails its apex workmanship burden where that binding reset protects or repairs the institutional relation but does not carry far enough through the legal routes by which the person governed by the change can still appear, challenge, prove, defend, obtain review, preserve the record, or receive a fitting answer.
That is not what happened everywhere in the executive-power cases.
The Court matched unreviewable administrative finality with superior review in Arthrex. It protected court and jury in Jarkesy and AT&T. It gave cause procedural content through notice, response, and review in the Federal Reserve setting. It stated a clear constitutional center for presidential supervision of ordinary executive administration.
The Court can carry.
That is why the places where it does not carry matter.
In presidential immunity, the Court identified a genuine threat to the constitutional office. It gave the former President an early hearing, categorical protection for exclusive authority, presumptive protection for other official acts, and an evidentiary shield against circumvention.
The Court carried the Presidency.
It carried the defendant.
It carried less fully the legal system’s capacity to examine unofficial conduct when the proof of that conduct was intertwined with protected presidential action. The case expected to develop that boundary disappeared. The doctrine survived without the adjudicatory record that might have shown how protection and proof could coexist.
In removal, the Court identified a genuine defect in the relationship between executive authority and presidential control. It invalidated the tenure restriction, preserved the agency, and clarified the constitutional rule for the future.
The Court carried presidential supervision.
It carried institutional continuity.
It carried less fully the officer who lost the period of service while litigation continued and the claimant required to prove that an unconstitutional control structure altered a particular agency action. The violation was categorical when institutional authority was at stake. Its consequence became conditional when the person asked what the violation meant for them.
That is the unevenness.
The finding concerns the structure released by the decisions, not the private motives of the Justices. 
The Court supplies its strongest work where the defect can be answered through a clear institutional reassignment: make the officer removable, give a superior final review, send the claim to court, or make cause judicially enforceable.
Its workmanship becomes thinner where no single reassignment completes the constitutional burden: where official and unofficial conduct intertwine, where evidence crosses the protected boundary, where retrospective consequence depends upon inaccessible proof, where time consumes the remedy, and where several institutions each carry only part of the public answer.
Those cases are harder.
Hardness is not a defense against the duty they create.
The Court need not eliminate uncertainty. It need not dictate outcomes. It need not write a complete code for every later dispute. But when its own judgment makes a new constitutional arrangement immediately operative, it must provide enough law for competent actors to administer that arrangement without allowing the human consequence to vanish into the distance among institutions.
The holdings remain binding where the workmanship is thin. Authority secures the reset; it does not prove that the reset has been carried. 
An agency may continue. An office may be filled. A prosecution may end. A doctrine may bind. A public controversy may move into elections and history.
The system remains active.
But constitutional government is not measured only by whether its institutions continue moving. It is also measured by whether the people governed through those institutions remain present in law.
A person may lose and still be carried.
The former President may receive immunity. The removed officer may possess no lawful right to reinstatement. The claimant may fail to prove causation. The government may prevail. A court may conclude that no remedy is available.
Those are legal answers.
The failure occurs where the route to an answer thins more quickly than the public authority imposing the consequence—where the person cannot enter, evidence cannot be used or tested, classification requires renewed apex judgment, remedy arrives after the interest has vanished, or the institution is repaired while the past remains unanswered. 
That is where binding constitutional law can cease to carry the human consequence of its own reset.
That is Apex Failure.
The term is deliberately bounded.
It does not describe the whole Supreme Court, every Roberts Court decision, or every instance of lawful doctrinal uncertainty. It describes the failure of apex constitutional workmanship where the Court changes the governing structure, carries the office or institution through the change, and leaves the person most exposed at the routes of proof, review, remedy, record, and answer.
This artifact does not finish the critique of the Roberts Court. Nor does it establish that the pattern identified here is unique across Supreme Court history. Those would require different records and different analyses.
It establishes a baseline.
A court’s workmanship cannot be judged only by the constitutional principle it announces at the center of the case. The judgment must also follow the principle into the legal order that must live under it.
Who acts next?
What law have they been given?
What evidence remains?
What happens to the prior action?
Can the affected person appear?
Which institution can answer?
And does that answer arrive while law can still make a difference?
Those questions do not weaken constitutional authority.
They are what constitutional authority owes after it acts.
A constitutional reset does not end when the Court issues its judgment. It enters institutions, prosecutions, offices, records, remedies, and lives. The Court need not decide every consequence in advance. But it must carry what it changes far enough that law can still answer for what follows.
The Roberts Court has shown that it can.
That is why, where it does not, the failure is apex failure.
